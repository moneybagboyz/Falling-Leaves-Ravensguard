# Ravensguard — Economy Design Reference

_An in-depth description of the simulation's economic model, written for designers, modders, and contributors who need to understand the system at a structural level._

---

## 1. Overview and Design Philosophy

Ravensguard's economy is a **closed-loop, agent-based supply-demand model** operating at the settlement level. It is not an abstraction layer that just adds numbers — every unit of wheat, every iron ingot, and every coin that appears in the world is created by a specific process, consumed by a specific actor, or physically moved between settlements by a trade party.

The design draws on three classical economic concepts simultaneously:

- **Physiocracy** — land and resource extraction are the root of all wealth. You cannot manufacture iron ingots you do not mine; you cannot bake bread from wheat that was never grown. The fertility of a cell and the resource tags on its terrain are the real constraints on any settlement's output.
- **Classical surplus theory** — settlements produce above their subsistence needs and route the surplus outward through trade. The trade network is the mechanism by which geographic comparative advantage is realized.
- **Demand-pull price dynamics** — prices are not player-set or administratively fixed. They rise when demand outpaces supply and fall when supply is abundant, with dampening to prevent wild oscillation.

There is deliberately no central bank, no monetary policy, and no global price floor imposed by the simulation. What stability exists is emergent from the interaction of production, consumption, and trade.

---

## 2. The Tick Architecture

The economy is driven by a **strategic cadence tick**, called `STRATEGIC_CADENCE`, defined in `TickScheduler`. Each strategic tick represents a bundle of in-game time. All economic mutations — production, consumption, price adjustment — happen once per strategic tick through the `SettlementPulse` system.

### 2.1 The Settlement Pulse Order

Every strategic tick, each settlement is processed in the following strict order:

| Step | System | Purpose |
|------|--------|---------|
| 1 | Shortage reset | Clears the prior pulse's shortage data so each pulse starts clean |
| 2 | Starter stock seed | On the very first tick, seeds a safety stock so the simulation does not immediately starve |
| 3a | `ProductionLedger.run` | Agriculture, extraction, and building-based recipes |
| 3b | Coin income | Each population class generates coin via wages, rents, and market fees |
| 4 | Consumption | Population classes draw from the inventory; shortfalls are recorded |
| 5 | `PriceLedger.update` | Prices adjust toward the current supply/demand equilibrium |
| 6 | Prosperity & unrest | Social stability indicators shift based on whether the population was fed |
| 7 | Worked-acre adjustment | Agricultural effort scales toward the prosperity-derived target |
| 8 | `TradePartySpawner.try_spawn_all` | Surplus settlements dispatch trade parties to shortage settlements |

This ordering is not arbitrary. Production must come before consumption (you cannot eat grain that has not yet been harvested this tick). Prices must be updated after consumption (so the shortage effect is reflected immediately in price). Trade parties are spawned last (after all settlements have run) so the spawner can evaluate the full world state when routing.

## 3. Goods

The game's tradeable commodities span four economic roles: **staple foods**, **raw materials**, **refined materials**, and **finished goods**. Coin is a special commodity serving as medium of exchange.

| Good | Base Value | Weight (kg) | Spoilage (days) | Category | Role |
|------|-----------|-------------|-----------------|----------|------|
| `wheat_bushel` | 4 | 27 | 180 | food_grain | Primary staple; driver of all consumption survival |
| `flour_sack` | 7 | 25 | 90 | food_processed | Processed grain; higher value, shorter shelf life |
| `timber_log` | 3 | 80 | — | raw_material | Construction input; smelter fuel; low value density |
| `iron_ore` | 5 | 8 | — | raw_material | Mining output; input to smelting chain |
| `iron_ingot` | 12 | 5 | — | refined_material | Smelted iron; universal industrial input |
| `iron_tools` | 15 | 3 | — | tool | Final manufactured good; high value density |
| `cloth_bolt` | 12 | 5 | — | cloth | Textile; consumed by merchants and nobles |
| `livestock_head` | 20 | 250 | — | food_livestock | Highest absolute value; logistically expensive |
| `coin` | 1 | 0.01 | — | currency | Medium of exchange; generated by population income |

### 3.1 Value Density and the Logistics Constraint

The ratio of `base_value` to `base_weight_kg` is the key signal for what trade parties _want_ to carry. Timber at 3 value per 80 kg (0.038 val/kg) is logistically expensive compared to iron tools at 15 value per 3 kg (5.0 val/kg). While the simulation does not yet enforce weight-based cargo limits, this ratio is built into every good's data and is available for future freight modeling.

### 3.2 Spoilage

Wheat and flour have explicit spoilage windows (180 and 90 days). Perishability creates urgency: a grain surplus in autumn is worth less to a distant settlement than the same surplus in spring, because by the time the trade party arrives, some fraction may have spoiled. This pressure incentivizes short-route, high-frequency trade over long-distance arbitrage for food goods.

## 4. Production

### 4.1 The Three Pathways

`ProductionLedger` implements three distinct production paradigms, reflecting the real structural differences between land-based, resource-based, and labour-based production.

#### 4.1.1 Acreage Production (Agriculture)

> _Recipe: `farm_grain` → output: `wheat_bushel`_

Agricultural output is a **Leontief-style fixed-coefficient function** of worked acreage, terrain fertility, and labour availability:

$$Q_{wheat} = A_{worked} \times Y_{base} \times F_{cell} \times \Lambda \times \Delta t$$

Where:
- $A_{worked}$ — worked acres this tick
- $Y_{base} = 0.11$ — base yield per acre per tick (from recipe data)
- $F_{cell} \in [0.05, 1.0]$ — terrain fertility of the settlement's cell (from world generation)
- $\Lambda \in [0.1, 2.0]$ — labour factor (see §4.2)
- $\Delta t$ — ticks elapsed since last pulse

This is explicitly Ricardian: differential land quality ($F_{cell}$) determines which settlements are most productive. A fertile plain settlement and a rocky hill settlement applying identical labour will produce radically different wheat outputs.

#### 4.1.2 Extraction Production (Logging, Mining)

> _Recipes: `log_timber` → `timber_log`; (future) `mine_iron` → `iron_ore`_

Extraction represents resource depletion from the environment. Unlike agriculture, extraction is gated by a **resource tag** on the settlement's terrain cell — you cannot log timber in a desert. The production function uses woodlot acreage as a proxy for available labour:

$$Q_{timber} = A_{woodlot} \times k_{labour} \times Y_{worker\_day} \times \Delta t$$

Where:
- $A_{woodlot}$ — woodlot acres allocated to the settlement
- $k_{labour} = 0.002$ — the `WOODLOT_LABOUR_FACTOR` constant; a fraction of woodlot acres that functions as effective worker-days
- $Y_{worker\_day} = 0.5$ — yield per worker-day (from recipe data)

The low `WOODLOT_LABOUR_FACTOR` deliberately keeps extraction modest in early phases, acknowledging that without a true labour model, deforestation or ore depletion would otherwise be implausibly fast.

#### 4.1.3 Standard (Building-Recipe) Production

Once buildings are constructed in a settlement, they unlock **standard recipes**: manufactured goods produced by converting inputs into higher-value outputs.

| Building | Recipe | Inputs | Output | Worker-days/batch |
|----------|--------|--------|--------|------------------|
| Grain Mill | `mill_grain` | 1× wheat_bushel | 1× flour_sack | 0.5 |
| Iron Smelter | `smelt_iron` | 2× iron_ore + 1× timber_log | 1× iron_ingot | 1 |
| Smithy | `forge_tools` | 3× iron_ingot + 1× timber_log | 1× iron_tools | 2 |

Standard recipes follow a **Leontief fixed-proportions production function**: the recipe runs zero batches if any single input is missing. There is no substitution. If a smelter has iron ore but no timber for charcoal, it runs idle — creating a real demand signal for timber in mineral-rich but forest-poor settlements.

The number of batches per pulse:

$$B = \frac{\Delta t}{W_{batch}}$$

Where $W_{batch}$ is the `worker_days_per_batch` from the recipe. All inputs are deducted and all outputs are credited atomically.

### 4.2 The Labour Factor

A **labour multiplier** $\Lambda$ scales all production relative to the population tier:

$$\Lambda = \text{clamp}\left(\frac{P_{total}}{P_{ref}[tier]}, 0.1, 2.0\right)$$

Reference populations by tier:

| Tier | Name | Seed range | Reference Pop |
|------|------|------------|---------------|
| 0 | Hamlet | 50–120 | 75 |
| 1 | Village | 150–500 | 300 |
| 2 | Town | 700–1,200 | 900 |
| 3 | City | 2,000–4,000 | 2,800 |
| 4 | Metropolis | 5,000–12,000 | 8,000 |

A Hamlet at exactly 75 people runs at $\Lambda = 1.0$. A flourishing over-populated Hamlet at 120 people runs at $\Lambda = 1.6$ — 60% bonus output. An underpopulated Hamlet at 7 people runs at $\Lambda = 0.1$ — 10% output. This creates a feedback loop: population growth drives production growth, but that production must also supply more mouths, which creates the incentive for trade.

The exponential tier gap (each tier ≈ 3–4× the previous) means cities and metropolises **cannot** feed themselves from their own territory and must import heavily from surrounding villages. This is the primary driver of inter-settlement trade in the simulation.

## 5. Population Classes and Demand

The demand side of the economy is driven by four **population classes**, each with distinct consumption requirements, income generation, and unrest sensitivity.

| Class | Wheat/head/tick | Cloth/head/tick | Coin Income/head/tick | Luxury Demand | Unrest per food shortage |
|-------|----------------|----------------|----------------------|---------------|------------------------|
| Peasant | 0.015 | — | 0.01 | 0% | 0.002 |
| Artisan | 0.015 | — | 0.05 | 10% | 0.003 |
| Merchant | 0.020 | 0.001 | 0.15 | 20% | 0.004 |
| Noble | 0.040 | 0.003 | 0.30 | 50% | 0.008 |

### 5.1 Structural Observations

**Wheat demand is stratified by class**: Peasants and artisans share the subsistence baseline (0.015 bu/head/tick). Merchants consume ~33% more (0.020) reflecting better-provisioned households. Nobles consume 2.7× the peasant rate (0.040), encompassing household servants, feasting, horse-fodder, and hospitality obligations — all of which flow through the grain ledger. This creates meaningful demand differentiation: a settlement with a large noble population draws down grain stocks far faster than a peasant village of equal total size.

**Coin income as a class proxy for economic activity**: The `coin_income_per_head_per_tick` values are not arbitrary — they encode the class's economic role. A noble generates 30× more coin income than a peasant because they collect rents, tax levies, and market tolls. A merchant at 15× the peasant rate reflects trading margins. These incomes are injected directly into the settlement's coin inventory each pulse, reflecting monetization of the local economy.

**Cloth as a luxury good for upper classes**: Only merchants and nobles consume cloth. This creates a structural demand for textile production in settlements with significant upper-class populations — a market dynamic that emerges organically rather than being scripted.

**Unrest sensitivity scales inversely with class resilience**: The noble class is the most politically fragile during food shortages (`unrest_per_food_shortage = 0.008`, 4× the peasant rate). This is economically significant: it models the reality that elites who expect reliable provisioning are quick to withdraw political support when shortfalls occur — more dangerous to a ruler than peasant grumbling.

## 6. The Price Mechanism

### 6.1 The Price Formation Formula

`PriceLedger` updates prices each pulse using a **demand-relative-to-supply ratio**, clamped and smoothed:

**Step 1 — Compute the target price:**

$$P_{target} = \text{clamp}\left(V_{base} \times \frac{D}{S}, \; V_{base} \times 0.25, \; V_{base} \times 4.0\right)$$

Where:
- $V_{base}$ — the good's base value from JSON data
- $D$ — estimated demand this pulse (population × consumption rate × $\Delta t$)
- $S$ — current inventory (floored at $\varepsilon = 0.001$ to prevent division by zero)

**Step 2 — Smooth toward target (momentum damping):**

$$P_{t+1} = P_t + 0.20 \times (P_{target} - P_t)$$

The 20% smoothing factor (`SMOOTH = 0.20`) means prices cannot gap from 1× to 4× base overnight. It takes roughly 7–8 pulses to traverse most of the price gap — a deliberate choice to prevent merchants from being able to perfectly arbitrage single-tick price spikes.

### 6.2 Price Bounds and Their Economic Meaning

| Bound | Value | Interpretation |
|-------|-------|---------------|
| Floor | 0.25× base | Structural price floor — commodity never sells for less than 25% of its reference value, preventing deflationary collapse even during glut conditions |
| Cap | 4.0× base | Crisis ceiling — price cannot exceed 4× base even during severe shortage, modeling price-gouging limits and substitution effects |

The floor and cap together create a **bounded volatility band**. A wheat bushel (base value 4) oscillates between 1 and 16 coin. An iron ingot (base 12) between 3 and 48. The gap is wide enough to create meaningful arbitrage incentives for trade but bounded enough to prevent the simulation from producing hyperinflationary spirals.

### 6.3 Demand Estimation

Demand is computed prospectively from population class definitions — not from actual consumption in this pulse. This is intentional: the price signal must represent expected demand, not just realized consumption (which would collapse demand to zero in a shortage, paradoxically lowering prices exactly when goods are most scarce).

$$D = \sum_{c \in classes} N_c \times r_{c,good} \times \Delta t$$

Where $N_c$ is the headcount of class $c$ and $r_{c,good}$ is that class's consumption rate per head per tick.

## 7. Prosperity and Unrest: The Social Feedback Loop

Prosperity ($\pi \in [0, 1]$) and unrest ($\mu \in [0, 1]$) are the settlement's social stability indicators. They are not cosmetic — they feed back into production through the worked-acre adjustment mechanism.

### 7.1 Prosperity Dynamics

$$\pi_{t+1} = \begin{cases} \min(\pi_t + 0.005, 1.0) & \text{if wheat shortfall} \leq 0.1 \\ \max(\pi_t - 0.008, 0.0) & \text{if wheat shortfall} > 0.1 \end{cases}$$

The asymmetry is deliberate: it takes 200 well-fed pulses to move from $\pi = 0$ to $\pi = 1.0$, but only 125 starving pulses to collapse from $\pi = 1.0$ to $\pi = 0$. This models the empirical tendency for social trust to accumulate slowly and erode quickly.

### 7.2 Unrest Dynamics

Unrest accumulates linearly with every consumption shortfall (scaled by the class-specific `unrest_per_food_shortage` rate) and decays at 0.003 per pulse when no food shortfall occurs:

$$\mu_{t+1} = \min\left(\mu_t + \sum_c N_c \cdot r_c \cdot \text{shortfall}_{c,food}, \; 1.0\right)$$

$$\mu_{t+1} = \max(\mu_t - 0.003, \; 0.0) \quad \text{(when no shortfall)}$$

At $\mu = 1.0$ the settlement is in declared crisis. Future systems (taxation, rebellion, population flight) will use this value as a trigger threshold.

### 7.3 The Prosperity-Agriculture Feedback Loop

Prosperity directly controls agricultural effort through the **worked-acre adjustment** each pulse:

$$A_{worked}^{target} = A_{arable} \times (0.40 + 0.50 \times \pi)$$

At $\pi = 0$ (misery): only 40% of arable land is actively farmed. At $\pi = 1.0$ (thriving): 90% is farmed. This is a stylized model of the real historical relationship between social stability and agricultural investment. Peasants in unstable, heavily taxed, or war-torn regions historically abandoned marginal lands, contracted their cultivation, and reverted to subsistence farming — the model captures that contraction.

The feedback loop it creates is:

> Shortage → Unrest rises, Prosperity falls → Fewer acres worked → Less wheat output → More shortage

This is a destabilizing positive feedback loop (in the systems-dynamics sense), which is historically accurate: medieval famines rarely resolved on their own without external intervention (trade imports, landlord grain releases, or population die-off reducing demand).

## 8. The Trade Network

### 8.1 Architecture

Trade is mediated by **trade parties** — physical agents that carry cargo from an origin settlement to a destination settlement along a route path. They are not teleport transactions. A trade party that has departed is _in transit_: the goods have been deducted from the origin's inventory, but have not yet arrived at the destination. This creates real economic lag in the system.

Each `TradePartyState` records:
- Origin and destination settlement IDs
- Cargo dictionary (good → quantity)
- Route path (sequence of map cells)
- Current path index (how far along the route they are)
- Speed (2.0 tiles per movement tick, default)
- Total ticks en route (for cost estimation in future freight models)

### 8.2 Spawn Logic: The Surplus-Seeking Mechanism

`TradePartySpawner` runs after all settlements have been pulsed each strategic tick. It implements a **decentralized price-signal-driven dispatch** mechanism — roughly analogous to how a medieval merchant house would scan known counterparties for profitable imbalances.

**Surplus detection threshold**: A settlement will only export a good if it holds more than 20 units above the `SURPLUS_THRESHOLD`. This prevents settlements from exporting down to zero and immediately starving.

**Destination scoring**: The spawner evaluates all directly connected settlements (by route edge) and scores them by need:

$$\text{score}_{dest} = \begin{cases} \text{shortage} \times 2.0 & \text{if shortage} > 5.0 \text{ (crisis resupply)} \\ (14 - \text{days\_of\_supply}) + 0.1 & \text{if days\_of\_supply} < 14 \text{ (proactive resupply)} \end{cases}$$

The two-tier scoring is notable: the crisis path (active shortage) doubles the urgency weight, reflecting that a destination already in active shortage is more valuable to serve than one that merely has a low buffer. The proactive path (14-day buffer check) means trade parties are dispatched _before_ a shortage becomes a crisis — a form of inventory management that models forward-looking merchant behavior.

**Cargo sizing**: The trade party carries 50% of the available surplus (`CARGO_FRACTION = 0.5`). Sending all surplus would eliminate the local buffer; sending too little would make the trip economically marginal. 50% is a conservative heuristic that keeps both the origin and the destination partially buffered.

**Deduplication**: Only one party per (origin, good, destination) triplet can be in transit at a time. This prevents redundant dispatch when a party is already en route.

### 8.3 Comparative Advantage in Practice

Because settlements have different `fertility` values, `resource_tags`, and population compositions, they naturally specialize. A high-fertility flatland settlement will consistently run wheat surpluses. A forest settlement with `wood` tags will run timber surpluses. A mountain settlement with `ore` tags will run iron ore surpluses.

The trade network converts this geographic heterogeneity into economic specialization. The flatland feeds the ironworks; the ironworks equips the farms with better tools; the timber camp fuels the smelters. This is the Heckscher-Ohlin theorem in miniature — factors of production (land quality, resource endowments) determine comparative advantage and the direction of trade flows.

## 9. The Monetary System

### 9.1 Coin Generation

Coin enters the settlement's inventory through `_generate_coin`, called each pulse:

$$C_{generated} = \sum_{c \in classes} N_c \times I_c \times \Delta t$$

Where $I_c$ is the `coin_income_per_head_per_tick` for class $c$. At a representative village (100 peasants, 20 artisans, 5 merchants, 2 nobles):

| Class | Headcount | Rate | Contribution/tick |
|-------|-----------|------|------------------|
| Peasant | 100 | 0.01 | 1.0 |
| Artisan | 20 | 0.05 | 1.0 |
| Merchant | 5 | 0.15 | 0.75 |
| Noble | 2 | 0.30 | 0.60 |
| **Total** | | | **3.35 coin/tick** |

Coin does not disappear when consumed in the `_consume` step because coin is not on any class's `consumption_per_head_per_tick` list (it is an income good, not a consumable). This is the correct model: coin circulates through the economy rather than being destroyed by spending. A future tax system will withdraw coin from settlements to simulate fiscal extraction, and a future wage system will redistribute coin to individual actors.

### 9.2 Coin as a Price Numeraire

All `base_value` figures are denominated in coin. The price mechanism always converts inventory levels and demand volumes back into coin-denominated prices. This means coin is not just a commodity — it is the unit of account. When a settlement's coin inventory is thin, it has limited capacity to purchase imports from trade parties (in the eventual market-price payment system), which creates a liquidity constraint that reinforces regional autarky in impoverished settlements.

## 10. Settlement Initialization and the Starter Stock

On the first pulse, if a settlement has no inventory, `_seed_starter_stock` is called:

$$I_{wheat}^{init} = \max(P_{total}, 10) \times 0.015 \times 30$$

$$I_{timber}^{init} = \max(A_{woodlot} \times 0.1, 5.0)$$

$$I_{coin}^{init} = (tier + 1) \times 20$$

The wheat seed is explicitly **30 days of supply** at baseline consumption — a deliberate choice to avoid an immediate famine before the first production run completes. The timber seed is proportional to woodlot access, acknowledging that a settlement with forest access should already have built up modest stocks. Coin scales with tier, acknowledging that larger settlements have historically accumulated capital.

## 11. Supply Chain Linkages and Economic Depth

The most economically interesting settlements are those embedded in multi-stage supply chains. The full iron-tools supply chain illustrates the depth:

```
Terrain cell with ore tag
        ↓
  [Iron Mine]  →  iron_ore  (0.8 units/worker-day)
        ↓
  [Iron Smelter]  +  timber_log  →  iron_ingot  (2 ore + 1 log → 1 ingot)
        ↓
  [Smithy]  +  timber_log  →  iron_tools  (3 ingots + 1 log → 1 tool)
        ↓
  Trade Route  →  Farming settlements
        ↓
  Better tools  →  Higher farm yield  (future: tool-boosted labour factor)
```

Each node in this chain creates a distinct demand signal. The smelter demands both ore (from mining) and timber (from logging). The smithy demands ingots and more timber. The farming settlement that imports tools will, in the future, produce more wheat — closing the circular flow.

At present, the demand for timber from iron production is real and active in the price mechanism — iron-producing settlements will bid up timber prices, creating trade flows of timber toward industrial centers.

## 12. Known Limitations and Future Work

| Limitation | Economic Consequence | Planned Resolution |
|-----------|---------------------|-------------------|
| No freight cost model | Trade parties are dispatched without route-length cost weighting; long-distance trade is as cheap as short-haul | Add travel cost in coin + time discount to trade scoring |
| No market institution gating | Settlements without a Market building still trade; markets have no price discovery bonus | Gate trade spawn rate on Market building presence |
| No seasonal production | Agriculture runs identically in all ticks regardless of season | Add seasonal yield multiplier keyed to calendar tick modulo |
| No population growth model | Population is static; labour factor cannot improve through fertility | Add birth/death/migration loop driven by prosperity and unrest |
| No tax extraction | Coin accumulates in settlements with no fiscal sink | Add faction tax collection as a coin drain tied to unrest thresholds |
| No tool-boosted yield | Iron tools exist and trade but do not improve farm output | Add `tool_factor` modifier to `_labour_factor` scaling |
| No wagon capacity limits | Trade parties carry cargo without weight or volume checks | Add per-party cargo capacity based on wagon count |
| No perishability enforcement | Spoilage days are recorded but not enforced in inventory | Add per-good spoilage decay on the inventory each pulse |
| Single-good party dispatch | Each trade party carries one good type | Allow multi-good manifest to simulate realistic caravan loads |
